AI Now Controls Your Desktop: Claude Can Click Your Mouse

The Industrial Scribe | AI Trends  ·  5 min read  ·  March 2026

On March 24, 2026, Anthropic announced that Claude can now directly control a user's computer — opening applications, navigating browsers, clicking buttons, filling spreadsheets, and managing files. No API integration required. No setup beyond granting permission.

AI tools no longer live inside a chat window. They now sit on your desktop, watching your screen, and completing tasks across any application you use. You can message Claude from your phone, and it will open Excel on your computer, process a file, save the output, and email it to your customer — while you're walking the factory floor.

This is the most significant shift in how non-technical professionals interact with AI since ChatGPT launched.

What Computer Use Means for Manufacturing

Before (Chat Only)Now (Computer Use)
Upload a file to the AI websiteAI opens the file directly on your computer
Copy an email, paste into AI, copy response backAI reads Outlook, drafts reply, you approve and send
Download AI output, open in PowerPoint, adjust manuallyAI opens PowerPoint, builds the deck, saves it
Describe what's on your screen in wordsAI sees your screen and acts on what it sees
One task at a time, in a browser tabMultiple tasks across multiple applications

The Practical Implications

Gartner predicts 40% of enterprise applications will include task-specific AI agents by the end of 2026. The race to automate desktop workflows is already underway. For manufacturing professionals, this means the AI tool you use for data analysis can now also prepare your presentations, manage your email, and organise your files — all within a single conversation.

A BD manager can now say: "Read the latest WIP export from my desktop, analyse customer growth, build a 5-slide presentation, and save it to my customer review folder." The AI does all of it without the human touching a mouse.

The Trust Question

Computer use requires granting AI access to your screen and inputs. Every action is visible, and the AI asks permission before performing sensitive operations. But the question manufacturers need to answer is: how much autonomy are you comfortable giving?

Start with low-risk tasks. File organisation, data formatting, presentation building. Build trust. Expand gradually. This technology is too powerful to ignore and too new to trust blindly.

The Bottom Line

The chat window was AI's training wheels. Computer use is AI moving into your actual workspace — your desktop, your applications, your workflow. Manufacturers who are still hesitating about whether to try a chatbot are now two generations behind.

The question is no longer "Should I chat with AI?" It's "Should I let AI operate my tools?" That question is coming for every manufacturing professional, whether they're ready for it or not.
