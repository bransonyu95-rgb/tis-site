AI Is Coming for Middle Management: Two Million Jobs at Risk

The Industrial Scribe | AI Trends  ·  5 min read  ·  March 2026

An MIT and Boston University study projects that AI will replace as many as two million manufacturing workers by 2026. But before factory floor workers start worrying, they should look at who's actually most at risk.

By the end of 2026, 20% of organisations will use AI to flatten their hierarchy, eliminating over 50% of current middle management positions. When broken down by seniority, middle management faces the highest vulnerability at 46%.

AI isn't coming for the machinist. It's coming for the person who manages the machinist's schedule, compiles the weekly report, and consolidates the delivery data.

Why Middle Management Is Vulnerable

Middle managers in manufacturing spend most of their time on three activities: collecting information from different sources, formatting it into reports or presentations, and passing it up to senior leadership with recommendations. All three are tasks that AI now handles faster and more consistently.

Customer business review deck1–2 daysUnder 10 minutesPresentation generation automated
Cross-departmental data consolidationHalf dayMinutesData fusion automated
Proposal / negotiation prep1–2 weeks1 afternoonDocument structuring automated

The Counterargument: AI Creates, Too

The numbers aren't entirely grim. While AI will displace an estimated two million manufacturing jobs by 2026, the World Economic Forum projects 170 million new jobs created globally by 2030 — a net gain of 78 million positions. And in manufacturing specifically, 2.1 million positions are already unfilled because there aren't enough workers.

The real story isn't that AI eliminates jobs. It's that AI eliminates tasks — and the people who defined their role by those tasks are the ones at risk. The BD manager who spends all week making PowerPoint slides is vulnerable. The BD manager who uses AI to make slides in 10 minutes and spends the rest of the week building customer relationships is not.

What Manufacturing Professionals Should Do

Don't compete with AI at tasks AI does better. Compete at what AI cannot do: understanding your customer's unspoken concerns, making judgment calls about which data matters, knowing that the delivery problem is really a scheduling problem in disguise, reading the room in a negotiation.

The professionals who survive the AI transition aren't the ones who avoid AI. They're the ones who use AI to eliminate the low-value work and spend more time on the high-value work that only humans can do. The goal isn't to do the same work faster. It's to do completely different — and more valuable — work with the time you get back.
